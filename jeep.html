Jeep (1940)

Versatile Military Vehicle

The Workhorse of War

Description and Major Uses

In the early 20th century, The Jeep was a general purpose vehicle developed by the US to replace horses for transportation of troops and equipment. No other vehicle at the time fit the US Army’s needs, so the Jeep was specially designed for military use. The Jeep fulfilled many roles during the war, these ranging from being the mount for cavalry units, the vehicle from which commanders led their units, hauling trailers full of vital supplies, carrying aircrew to their aircraft, carrying litters bearing wounded troops, transporting generals and admirals to work, or pulling light artillery pieces into battle.

Origin and Creator

The Jeep was the result of a request by the Army in 1940 for a general purpose vehicle that was small, lightweight, had four-wheel drive for cross-country travel, and capable of carrying a light machine gun. Initially, only two companies provided vehicles to meet this requirement: American Bantam and Willys-Overland. After the initial tests, the Army asked for changes and also opened the door to other manufacturers, notably Ford, for further testing and development in late 1940 and into 1941



Fun Fact

The nickname Jeep came from soldiers slurring the acronym GP (which stands for “General Purpose”) being used commonly to describe the vehicle. Willys-Overland would soon copyright the name after it was popularized by a journalist’s story.
