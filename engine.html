Jet Engine (1939)

Revolutionizing Aircraft Propulsion

Speed and Power Take Flight

Description and Major Uses

A jet engine works quite differently than earlier engines. A jet engine burns fuel and the exhaust gasses are released in one direction, which drives the engine in the opposite direction. This is explained by Newton’s third law of motion (for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction). Jet aircraft, used principally as fighters, had a tremendous speed advantage over existing planes, but had limited range and less-than-ideal handling. Moreover, they were not produced in sufficient numbers to have a large effect on the course of the war. The first U.S jet was the Bell P-59A Airacomet, completed in 1941. Other jet aircraft followed before the end of the war, such as Germany’s famous Messerschmitt Me 262.

Origin and Creator

The idea of a jet engine occurred first to Englishman and aeronautical student Frank Whittle in 1928. However, three German engineers had arrived at the same concept independently in the early and mid-1930s. By 1937 Frank Whittle was able to demonstrate his idea in the laboratory, and in 1941 the British Royal Air Force began using its first jet aircraft, the Gloster E.28/39. But a young German physicist, Hans von Ohain, went on to develop the world's first jet plane, the experimental Heinkel He 178. It first flew on August 27, 1939 beating the British to the invention of the jet plane.
